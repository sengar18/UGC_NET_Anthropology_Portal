Week 1 — Human Evolution & Paleoanthropology (Days 1–7)

Core Content to Master

  • Evolutionary sequence: Sahelanthropus → Ardipithecus → Australopithecus (afarensis, africanus, robustus, boisei) → Homo habilis → Homo erectus → archaic Homo sapiens → Homo sapiens
  • Key fossil discoveries: Lucy (AL-288-1), Taung Child, Turkana Boy, Peking Man, Java Man — know the discoverer, site, year, and morphological features
  • Bipedalism: anatomical evidence, hypotheses (energetics, thermoregulation, freeing hands), vs knuckle-walking
  • Brain size increase (encephalization quotient) and its anthropological implications
  • Out of Africa hypothesis vs Multiregional continuity — evidence, key proponents, current consensus
  • Neanderthals: morphology, culture (Mousterian), interbreeding evidence with modern humans
  • Denisovans: discovery, genetic overlap, geographic range
  • Stone tool industries: Oldowan → Acheulean → Mousterian → Upper Palaeolithic sequence
⏱ 3 hrs/day theory + build a fossil timeline chart on Day 4

PYQ-Targeted Sub-Topics

  • Brain-to-body ratio comparisons across hominid species
  • Which fossil was found first / which is oldest / which showed what morphological "first"
  • Cranial capacity ranges: A. afarensis ~450cc → H. habilis ~600cc → H. erectus ~900cc → H. sapiens ~1350cc
  • Site-species-age triangles: Turkana (Kenya), Olduvai Gorge (Tanzania), Hadar (Ethiopia), Sterkfontein (S. Africa)
  • Foramen magnum position as indicator of bipedalism
  • Difference: gracile vs robust australopithecines — dental morphology, sagittal crest, diet
📝 Create flash cards for each fossil — 1 card = 1 species
Week 2 — Genetics, Race & Biological Variation (Days 8–14)

Genetics Core

  • Mendelian genetics: dominant/recessive, codominance, incomplete dominance — with anthropological examples
  • Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium: formula p²+2pq+q²=1 and p+q=1, five conditions, applications in population studies
  • Microevolutionary forces: mutation, natural selection, genetic drift, gene flow, non-random mating
  • ABO blood group system: alleles (Iᴬ, Iᴮ, i), frequencies across world populations, forensic application
  • Other blood group systems: Rh factor, MN system, Diego antigen
  • Sickle cell anaemia: malaria hypothesis (heterozygous advantage), distribution map, mechanism
  • G6PD deficiency, Tay-Sachs, PKU — as examples of genetic disorders in specific populations
  • mtDNA and Y-chromosome studies: matrilineal vs patrilineal lineages, Mitochondrial Eve, Y-chromosome Adam
⏱ 2.5 hrs theory + 20 MCQs from genetics PYQs each day

Race, Variation & Osteology

  • Biological concept of race vs social construct — UNESCO statements (1950, 1951, 1964)
  • Clines and clinal distribution of traits — vs typological race concept
  • Major racial classifications: Hooton, Coon, Garn, Birdsell
  • Indian racial types: Negrito, Proto-Australoid, Mongoloid, Mediterranean, Western Brachycephals, Nordic (Guha's classification)
  • Basic osteology: skull landmarks (nasion, glabella, opisthocranion, basion), long bone structure, epiphyseal fusion for age estimation
  • Sexual dimorphism in skeletal remains — pelvic morphology, mastoid process, sciatic notch
  • Body build indices: cephalic index, nasal index, stature estimation from long bones
📝 Memorize Guha's 6 Indian racial types with geographic distribution
Week 3 — Primatology (Days 15–21)

Primate Taxonomy & Morphology

  • Order Primates → Suborders: Strepsirhini (prosimians: lemurs, lorises, galagos) and Haplorhini (tarsiers + anthropoids)
  • Platyrrhini (New World monkeys: wide nose, prehensile tail) vs Catarrhini (Old World monkeys + apes + humans)
  • Superfamilies: Cercopithecoidea (OW monkeys), Hominoidea (gibbons, orangutans, gorillas, chimpanzees, humans)
  • Primate characteristics: binocular vision, prehensile hands, nails vs claws, large brain-to-body ratio, long life history
  • Locomotion types: brachiation, knuckle-walking, bipedalism, vertical clinging & leaping, quadrupedalism
  • Dental formula comparison: prosimians, OW monkeys (2.1.2.3), humans (2.1.2.3)
⏱ 2 hrs theory + 15 MCQs per day from primatology PYQs

Primate Behaviour & Social Organisation

  • Social structures: solitary (orangutan), pair bond (gibbon), one-male (gorilla), multi-male (chimpanzee, baboon)
  • Dominance hierarchies, grooming coalitions, kin selection in primates
  • Tool use: Jane Goodall's chimpanzee research at Gombe — termite fishing, leaf sponging
  • Language acquisition experiments: Washoe, Koko, Kanzi — sign language and symbol use
  • Primate communication: calls, gestures, facial expressions vs human language
  • Infanticide, allomothering, sexual swelling and mate selection
  • Key researchers: Dian Fossey (gorillas), Birutė Galdikas (orangutans), Jane Goodall (chimpanzees)

Biological Anthropology — High-Yield One-Liners

Week 5 — History of Anthropological Theory (Days 31–37)

Classical & Evolutionary Schools

  • Unilinear Evolution: Spencer (Survival of Fittest in society), Tylor (concept of Animism, Survivals, culture defined), Morgan (Savagery→Barbarism→Civilization, kinship systems in Ancient Society), Frazer (magic→religion→science, sympathetic magic)
  • Diffusionism: British school (Smith, Perry — heliocentrism, cradle of civilisation in Egypt), German-Austrian school (Ratzel, Frobenius, Kulturkreis), American diffusionism (Wissler, culture area concept)
  • Historical Particularism: Franz Boas — critique of evolution, cultural relativism, fieldwork method, 4-field approach. Students: Kroeber, Lowie, Sapir, Mead, Benedict
  • Culture & Personality: Mead (Coming of Age in Samoa, Sex and Temperament), Benedict (Patterns of Culture — Apollonian vs Dionysian), Kardiner (basic personality structure)
⏱ 3 hrs theory — build a chronological theory-table as you go, each school as one row

Functionalism to Structuralism

  • Malinowski: Biopsychological functionalism, seven basic needs theory, Argonauts of the Western Pacific, Kula ring, Crime and Custom in Savage Society
  • Radcliffe-Brown: Structural functionalism, social structure vs culture, synchronic analysis, Andaman Islanders, kinship as social structure
  • Lévi-Strauss: Structuralism, binary oppositions (raw/cooked, nature/culture), myth analysis (Mythologiques), Elementary Structures of Kinship, Totemism, Savage Mind
  • Parsons & Merton: Functional prerequisites, manifest vs latent functions, functional equivalents
  • Symbolic Anthropology: Turner (liminality, communitas, Symbols in Ndembu Ritual), Geertz (thick description, interpretive anthropology, Religion as a Cultural System)
📝 Theory MCQ drill: 30 Qs daily from theory PYQs 2013–2024
Week 6 — Modern & Contemporary Theory (Days 38–44)

Neo-Evolutionism & Marxist Anthropology

  • White: Cultural evolution driven by energy capture (E×T×V), Basic Law of Cultural Evolution
  • Steward: Multilinear evolution, cultural ecology, culture core, levels of sociocultural integration
  • Sahlins & Service: Band, tribe, chiefdom, state typology; Stone Age Economics; Original Affluent Society
  • Marxist Anthropology: Meillassoux, Terray, Godelier — mode of production in pre-capitalist societies; domestic mode of production
  • Feminist Anthropology: Rosaldo, Ortner, Leacock, Slocum — critiques of androcentrism; women the gatherer hypothesis; public/domestic dichotomy
⏱ 2.5 hrs theory + review theory table daily for 20 minutes

Postmodernism, Post-colonialism & Applied Schools

  • Postmodern Anthropology: Clifford & Marcus (Writing Culture), reflexivity, ethnographic authority, crisis of representation
  • Processual Anthropology: Barth (ethnic groups and boundaries, transactionalism), Bailey, Boissevain
  • Bourdieu: Habitus, field, capital (economic, cultural, social, symbolic), practice theory, Outline of a Theory of Practice
  • Foucault's influence: Power/knowledge, discourse, governmentality — applied by anthropologists like Rabinow
  • World Systems & Globalization: Wallerstein (core-periphery), Wolf (Europe and the People Without History), Appadurai (scapes theory — ethnoscapes, mediascapes, technoscapes, financescapes, ideoscapes)
📝 Weekly review: test yourself by writing key concept of each theorist from memory
Week 7 — Kinship, Marriage & Social Organisation (Days 45–51)

Kinship Fundamentals

  • Descent systems: unilineal (patrilineal, matrilineal), bilateral, double/bilineal, ambilineal — examples for each
  • Descent groups: lineage vs clan vs phratry vs moiety vs sib
  • Kinship terminology systems: Eskimo, Hawaiian, Iroquois, Crow, Omaha, Sudanese — what each lumps and splits
  • Alliance theory (Lévi-Strauss, Needham) vs Descent theory (Fortes, Evans-Pritchard) — key debate
  • Elementary vs complex structures of kinship
  • Pitt-Rivers rule, genealogical distance, classificatory vs descriptive terminology (Morgan)
⏱ Draw diagrams for each kinship terminology system — visual learning is essential here

Marriage Systems

  • Cross-cousin vs parallel cousin marriage — which is preferred where and why (alliance theory)
  • Bilateral cross-cousin marriage, matrilateral cross-cousin marriage, patrilateral cross-cousin marriage
  • Levirate (widow marries deceased husband's brother) vs Sororate (widower marries deceased wife's sister)
  • Hypergamy, hypogamy, isogamy — social stratification implications
  • Polygyny vs polyandry (fraternal polyandry — Toda, Tibet examples)
  • Bride price (bridewealth) vs dowry — economic and social functions
  • Exogamy vs endogamy — gotra exogamy in India, village endogamy in North India
  • Ghost marriage, woman-to-woman marriage among Nuer
📝 Make a marriage-type identification table: 20 practice scenarios
Week 8 — Indian Anthropology & Tribal Studies (Days 52–60)

Tribal India — Fundamentals

  • Definition of tribe in Indian context — debate between tribe as a category vs communities
  • Scheduled Tribes: 705+ communities, constitutional definition under Article 342
  • PVTGs (Particularly Vulnerable Tribal Groups): 75 groups identified — know at least 25 with their states, population characteristics, and vulnerability criteria
  • Geographic distribution: North-East (Naga, Mizo, Khasi, Garo, Bodo), Central India (Gond, Bhil, Oraon, Munda, Santhal, Ho), South (Irula, Toda, Kota, Kurumba, Chenchu), Islands (Jarawa, Sentinelese, Onge)
  • Tribal economy: shifting cultivation (jhum), hunting-gathering, pastoralism, artisanal crafts
  • Tribal social structure: clan system, totemism, age grades, village councils (gram sabha)
⏱ 3 hrs for tribal content — memorise PVTG list using a state-wise mental map

Tribal Movements & Constitutional Provisions

  • Tribal Movements: Santhal Hul (1855–56), Birsa Munda's Ulgulan (1899–1900), Tana Bhagat Movement, Bodo Movement, Naga political movements
  • Fifth Schedule: Scheduled areas, Tribes Advisory Councils, Governor's powers — applies to 10 states
  • Sixth Schedule: Autonomous District Councils in NE — Assam, Meghalaya, Tripura, Mizoram
  • PESA 1996: Panchayats (Extension to Scheduled Areas) Act — gram sabha powers, minor forest produce rights
  • Forest Rights Act 2006: Individual Forest Rights, Community Forest Rights, habitat rights for PVTGs
  • Applied Anthropology in India: Tribal development schemes, Eklavya Model Residential Schools, Van Dhan Vikas Kendras, TRIFED

Week 9 — Archaeological Anthropology: Prehistoric India (Days 61–67)

Stone Age Cultures of India

  • Lower Palaeolithic: Sohan Culture (Punjab), Madrasian Culture (South India); tools — hand axes, cleavers, choppers; sites: Attirampakkam, Bhimbetka (rock art)
  • Middle Palaeolithic: Levallois technique, flake tools; sites: Nevasa, Chirki
  • Upper Palaeolithic: Blade and burin tools, bone tools first appear; sites: Renigunta, Belan Valley
  • Mesolithic: Microlithic tools, geometric microliths; sites: Langhnaj (Gujarat), Bagor (Rajasthan), Adamgarh (M.P.), Sarai Nahar Rai (UP); semi-settled, domesticated dog
  • Neolithic: Polished stone tools, pottery, agriculture, animal domestication; North India (Burzahom, Gufkral in Kashmir — pit dwellings), South India (Brahmagiri, Piklihal, Paiyampalli — ash mounds), NE India (Daojali Hading)
  • Chalcolithic: Copper + stone; Kayatha, Malwa, Jorwe, Ahar-Banas cultures
⏱ Build period-site-tool-feature table — this is your single most important archaeology reference

Indus Valley Civilisation

  • Phase: Early Harappan (3500–2600 BCE), Mature (2600–1900 BCE), Late/Decline (1900–1300 BCE)
  • Key sites: Harappa (Ravi, Punjab — first found 1921, Daya Ram Sahni), Mohenjo-daro (Sindh — R.D. Banerji), Dholavira (Gujarat — R.S. Bisht — unique water conservation), Lothal (Gujarat — A.R. Rao — dockyard), Kalibangan (Rajasthan — fire altars), Rakhigarhi (largest IVC site, Haryana)
  • Urban features: grid planning, Great Bath, granaries, drainage system, standardised weights & measures, script (undeciphered)
  • Decline theories: Aryan invasion (Wheeler — now largely discredited), climate change/drought (Gwen Robbins Schug), flooding, trade route shifts
  • Significant absence: no evidence of temples, palaces, or clear royal tombs
📝 Site-excavator-special feature triples are almost always tested — commit to memory
Week 10 — Dating Methods & Iron Age to Historical Period (Days 68–74)

Archaeological Dating Methods

  • Relative dating: Stratigraphy (law of superposition), Typology (tool or pottery style evolution), Fluorine/nitrogen/uranium absorption test (for bones), Pollen analysis (palynology), Cross dating
  • Absolute/Chronometric dating: Radiocarbon (C-14): half-life 5730 years, range up to ~50,000 BP, Willard Libby (Nobel Prize). Dendrochronology (tree rings). Potassium-Argon (K-Ar): for volcanic rocks, range millions of years. Thermoluminescence (TL): for ceramics and sediments. Uranium series: for coral and cave formations. Archaeomagnetic dating. OSL (Optically Stimulated Luminescence)
  • Key fact: C-14 works only on organic material; K-Ar works only on volcanic rock
  • Patination and weathering: relative, qualitative only
⏱ Dating methods appear in almost every paper — know each method's material, range, and principle

Iron Age & Megalithic Cultures + Excavation Concepts

  • Megalithic cultures of South India: dolmens, cairn circles, menhirs, port-hole cists; associated with Iron Age; sites: Brahmagiri, Adichanallur, Hallur
  • Gangetic Iron Age — Painted Grey Ware (PGW, 1200–600 BCE) and Northern Black Polished Ware (NBP, 600–200 BCE)
  • Key excavation concepts: Wheeler's box-grid system, horizontal vs vertical excavation, context/locus, feature, assemblage
  • Ceramic seriation: a key relative dating technique
  • Ethnoarchaeology: using living societies to interpret archaeological remains (Binford)
  • New / Processual Archaeology: Binford — culture as adaptive system, middle range theory
  • Post-processual archaeology: Hodder — contextual, interpretive approach, Back from the Edge
📝 Draw a timeline: 50,000 BCE → present with cultures mapped on it
Week 11 — Research Methods, Statistics & Applied Anthropology (Days 75–81)

Research Methods in Anthropology

  • Fieldwork: Malinowski as pioneer — prolonged stay, learning local language, participant observation
  • Participant observation: emic vs etic perspectives; reflexivity; observer's paradox
  • Ethnographic methods: interview (structured, semi-structured, unstructured), genealogical method (Rivers), life history method, case study, network analysis
  • PRA (Participatory Rural Appraisal) and RRA (Rapid Rural Appraisal) — tools used in development projects
  • Sampling: random, stratified, purposive, snowball, quota — when to use each
  • Triangulation: using multiple methods to validate findings
  • Ethics: informed consent, anonymity, IRB approval, FPIC (Free Prior Informed Consent) for tribal communities
⏱ 1.5 hrs theory — focus on concept identification, not deep theory

Basic Statistics & Applied Anthropology

  • Measures of central tendency: mean, median, mode — when each is appropriate
  • Measures of dispersion: range, standard deviation, variance
  • Chi-square test: for categorical data association, formula χ²=Σ(O-E)²/E, degrees of freedom
  • Correlation coefficient (Pearson's r): strength and direction of linear relationship
  • Null hypothesis vs alternative hypothesis; p-value; Type I and Type II errors
  • Applied Anthropology: Action anthropology (Tax), Development anthropology, Advocacy anthropology, Policy-oriented research
  • Forensic anthropology applications: skeletal identification, age/sex/ancestry estimation, trauma analysis, bite mark analysis
  • Medical anthropology: explanatory models of illness (Kleinman), ethno-medicine, biocultural approach to disease
📝 Practice 5 chi-square calculations manually — understand the logic, not just the formula

Trap Question Patterns to Know

  • Alliance theory vs descent theory proponents — Lévi-Strauss = Alliance; Fortes/Evans-Pritchard = Descent
  • Ethnography of Speaking (Hymes) vs Ethnomethodology (Garfinkel) — commonly confused

Hominid Evolutionary Timeline

~7 MYA — Chad

Sahelanthropus tchadensis

Oldest known hominid; found by Michel Brunet; small canines, foramen magnum position suggests bipedalism; nicknamed "Toumaï"

~6 MYA — Kenya

Orrorin tugenensis

Found by Brigitte Senut & Martin Pickford; femoral anatomy suggests bipedalism; called "Millennium Man"

~4.4 MYA — Ethiopia

Ardipithecus ramidus

Found by Tim White; "Ardi" specimen (ARA-VP-6/500); facultative biped; small canines; woodland habitat (challenges savanna hypothesis)

~3.9–2.9 MYA — Ethiopia, Tanzania

Australopithecus afarensis

"Lucy" (AL-288-1) found by Donald Johanson; knee joint confirms bipedalism; laetoli footprints (3.6 MYA); small brain ~450cc; Hadar, Ethiopia; gracile

~3.3–2.1 MYA — South Africa

Australopithecus africanus

"Taung Child" found by Raymond Dart (1924); first African hominid fossil; gracile; Sterkfontein, Makapansgat sites

~2.7–1.2 MYA — East & South Africa

Paranthropus robustus & boisei

Robust australopithecines; massive sagittal crest; large molars for tough plant food; P. boisei found by Mary Leakey at Olduvai (1959) — "Nutcracker Man" / Zinjanthropus

~2.4–1.4 MYA — East Africa

Homo habilis

First Homo; "handy man"; associated with Oldowan tools; ~600cc brain; found by Louis & Mary Leakey at Olduvai Gorge; Homo habilis formal name by Leakey, Tobias & Napier (1964)

~1.89–0.14 MYA — Africa, Asia, Europe

Homo erectus

Java Man (Dubois, 1891) & Peking Man (Davidson Black, 1927); first to leave Africa; Acheulean tools; controlled fire; ~900cc brain; Sangiran site (Java), Zhoukoudian (China)

~700,000–200,000 BP

Archaic Homo sapiens / H. heidelbergensis

Transitional; Mauer mandible (Germany, 1907); Boxgrove (UK); large brain ~1200cc; used Acheulean tools; hunted large game; ancestor of both Neanderthals and modern humans

~430,000–40,000 BP — Europe, W. Asia

Homo neanderthalensis

Neanderthal Valley, Germany (1856); robust build for cold climate; Mousterian tools; evidence of burial, ochre use, symbolic behavior; 1–4% DNA in non-African modern humans (interbreeding proven)

~300,000 BP — Present

Homo sapiens

Jebel Irhoud, Morocco (oldest H. sapiens, ~315,000 BP); Omo Kibish, Ethiopia (~195,000 BP); fully modern behavior ~70,000 BP (Upper Palaeolithic revolution); global dispersal from Africa

Evolutionary Trends to Memorise

Brain Size Progression

A. afarensis (~450cc) → H. habilis (~600cc) → H. erectus (~900cc) → H. heidelbergensis (~1200cc) → H. neanderthalensis (~1450cc) → H. sapiens (~1350cc avg)

Note: Neanderthals had LARGER average brain than modern humans

Tool Industries Sequence

Oldowan (Mode 1) → Acheulean (Mode 2) → Mousterian (Mode 3) → Upper Palaeolithic (Mode 4: blades) → Microlithic (Mode 5)

Oldowan = H. habilis | Acheulean = H. erectus | Mousterian = Neanderthal

Anatomical Evolutionary Trends

Increased cranial capacity · Reduced prognathism · Reduction in tooth and jaw size · Loss of sagittal crest · Chin development (only in H. sapiens) · Foramen magnum shifts to center · Brow ridges reduce · Femur angles change for bipedalism

Bipedalism Evidence

Valgus knee, angled femur, adducted hallux, arched foot, S-curved spine, bowl-shaped pelvis, centrally-placed foramen magnum, shorter arms relative to legs

Laetoli footprints (3.6 MYA) = earliest confirmed bipedalism evidence

Out of Africa — Key Evidence

Mitochondrial DNA studies show common female ancestor ~150,000 BP in Africa. Y-chromosome Adam ~200,000+ BP. Complete replacement model vs admixture model. Modern non-Africans carry 1–4% Neanderthal DNA — proving some interbreeding.

Genetics & Molecular Anthropology — Concept Reference

All key formulae, concepts and facts needed for genetics questions

Hardy-Weinberg Equilibrium

Allele frequencies remain constant generation to generation in absence of evolutionary forces. Five conditions: no mutation, random mating, no natural selection, large population, no gene flow

p² + 2pq + q² = 1  |  p + q = 1

ABO Blood Group System

Three alleles: Iᴬ (dominant), Iᴮ (dominant), i (recessive). Type AB = codominance. Universal donor: O (ii). Universal recipient: AB. Rh+ indicates presence of D antigen.

Iᴬ Iᴬ or Iᴬ i = Type A  |  Iᴮ Iᴮ or Iᴮ i = Type B

Sickle Cell Anaemia

HbS mutation: valine substitutes glutamic acid in beta-globin chain. HbAS (sickle cell trait): resistant to malaria. HbSS: severe anaemia. High frequency in sub-Saharan Africa and parts of India (Odisha, M.P.) — balancing selection.

Heterozygote advantage = Balanced Polymorphism

Genetic Drift

Random change in allele frequency in small populations. Founder Effect: new population from small group (eg: Amish, island populations). Bottleneck Effect: population crash reduces genetic diversity.

Effect ↑ as population size ↓

mtDNA & Y-Chromosome

mtDNA inherited only from mother — traces matrilineal lineage. Y-chromosome inherited only from father — traces patrilineal lineage. mtDNA Eve: ~150,000–200,000 BP, East Africa. Y-chromosome Adam: ~200,000–340,000 BP.

mtDNA mutation rate ~2–4% per million years

Natural Selection Types

Directional selection: shifts mean towards one extreme. Stabilising selection: favours average phenotype (most common). Disruptive selection: favours two extreme phenotypes, against middle. Sexual selection: Darwin's mechanism.

Fitness (w) = relative reproductive success

Gene Flow

Transfer of alleles between populations through migration. Reduces genetic differences between populations. Clines (gradual variation in allele frequency over geographic distance) largely result from gene flow. Example: Duffy antigen (malaria resistance) cline in Africa.

More gene flow → populations more similar

Consanguinity & Inbreeding

Coefficient of inbreeding (F): probability that both alleles at a locus are identical by descent. First cousin marriage: F = 0.0625. Uncle-niece: F = 0.125. Effects: increased homozygosity, expression of recessive disorders (inbreeding depression).

First cousins share 12.5% of genes on average

Archaeological Dating Methods — Visual Reference

Every method: type · material it works on · range · principle · exam gotchas

Relative Dating
Absolute / Chronometric Dating
🏛

Stratigraphy

Relative
MaterialSediments, soil layers, archaeological deposits
PrincipleLaw of Superposition — lower layers are older than upper layers (unless disturbed)
RangeUnlimited — works across geological and archaeological timescales
Key NamesNicolas Steno (1669) — formulated the law; William Smith — applied to fossils
Exam TipMost fundamental dating principle; inverted stratigraphy (disturbance) is a frequent trap question
🔧

Typology / Seriation

Relative
MaterialStone tools, pottery, art styles, artefact assemblages
PrincipleArtefact styles change over time in a recognisable pattern — style evolution
RangeUnlimited — relative sequence only, no absolute dates
Key NamesOscar Montelius (typological method); Flinders Petrie (ceramic seriation in Egypt)
Exam TipPetrie's battleship curve is the classic seriation example; tool mode sequence (1→5) is typology in action
🦷

Fluorine-Uranium-Nitrogen Test

Relative
MaterialBones and teeth — organic remains from the SAME deposit only
PrincipleFluorine & uranium increase over time in bone; nitrogen decreases over time. Compare levels between specimens.
RangeCannot give age in years — only tells if specimens are contemporaneous
Key NamesKenneth Oakley used it to expose the Piltdown Man fraud (1949)
Exam TipPiltdown Man hoax detection via FUN test = most famous application. Works only on bones from the SAME deposit.
🌿

Palynology (Pollen Analysis)

Relative
MaterialPollen grains preserved in peat bogs, lake sediments, cave floors
PrinciplePollen types change with climate and vegetation changes — creates a relative sequence (pollen diagram)
RangeHundreds to millions of years — relative sequence only without corroboration
Key NamesLennart von Post — developed pollen analysis method, 1916 (Sweden)
Exam TipUsed to reconstruct past vegetation and climate (paleoclimate); valuable for understanding human adaptations to environment
⚛

Radiocarbon Dating (C-14)

Absolute
MaterialOrganic material only — bone, charcoal, wood, shell, seeds, peat
PrincipleC-14 is absorbed while alive; after death it decays at a known rate. Measure remaining C-14 to calculate age.
Half-life5,730 years (corrected value) — Libby's original: 5,568 years. Both values appear in exams.
RangeUp to ~50,000 BP (about 8–9 half-lives); AMS technique extends to ~75,000 BP
Key NamesWillard F. Libby — developed method 1949; Nobel Prize in Chemistry 1960
Exam TipDoes NOT work on rock, metal, or inorganic material. Calibration needed due to varying atmospheric C-14. Most exam favourite absolute method.
🌋

Potassium-Argon (K-Ar)

Absolute
MaterialVolcanic rock only (lava, tuff, igneous rock). NOT for organic material.
PrincipleRadioactive K-40 decays to Ar-40 at known rate. Measure trapped argon to calculate age of rock formation.
Half-life1.25 billion years — suitable for very old materials
Range100,000 years to billions of years — used for Pliocene/Miocene hominid sites
Exam TipUsed to date VOLCANIC ROCK surrounding fossils (not the fossil itself). Key sites: Olduvai, Hadar, Laetoli. Ar-Ar variant is more precise.
🌲

Dendrochronology

Absolute
MaterialWood — timber in ancient structures, preserved wood, wooden artefacts
PrincipleTrees form one ring per year; ring width varies with climate. Match patterns to master chronology to get exact year.
RangeUp to ~12,000 years BP (master chronology for European oaks)
Key NamesA.E. Douglass — developed dendrochronology; Andrew Ellicott Douglass (1920s)
Exam TipMost precise dating method (can give exact year); also provides palaeoclimate data. Limitation: needs preserved wood AND master chronology for that region.
🏺

Thermoluminescence (TL)

Absolute
MaterialHeated materials — pottery, burned flint, baked clay, hearth stones
PrincipleElectrons trapped by radiation since last heating. When reheated in lab, they release light proportional to accumulated dose.
Range5,000 to 500,000 years BP
Exam TipOnly works on material that was PREVIOUSLY HEATED. Used when C-14 and K-Ar are both unsuitable. OSL (Optically Stimulated Luminescence) is the light-based equivalent for sediments.
🪸

Uranium Series Dating

Absolute
MaterialCalcium carbonate — cave formations (stalactites, stalagmites), coral, travertine, tooth enamel
PrincipleU-238 and U-235 decay through a series of radioactive isotopes; measure ratio of parent to daughter isotopes
Range1,000 to ~500,000 years BP (U-Th); U-Pa extends to ~600,000 BP
Exam TipUseful for cave sites where C-14 fails beyond 50,000 BP. Used to date Neanderthal and early H. sapiens cave sites in Europe and Middle East.
🧬

Molecular Clock / DNA Dating

Absolute
MaterialAncient DNA (aDNA) from bones and teeth; modern comparative DNA
PrincipleDNA mutates at roughly constant rate; number of mutations between two sequences estimates time since divergence
Range100s of years to millions of years (molecular clock); aDNA extraction up to ~800,000 BP
Key NamesSvante Pääbo (Nobel 2022) — Neanderthal & Denisovan genomes; Emile Zuckerkandl & Linus Pauling — molecular clock concept
Exam TipUsed for mtDNA Eve (~200,000 BP), Y-chromosome Adam, and Neanderthal–Denisovan–H. sapiens split estimates. Increasingly tested in recent papers.
🧲

Archaeomagnetism

Absolute
MaterialFired clay, baked earth, hearths, kilns — in situ (unmoved since original heating)
PrincipleIron particles in clay align with Earth's magnetic field when heated. Earth's magnetic pole wanders — recorded pole position dates the firing event.
RangeUp to ~10,000 years BP (limited by available master polar wander curve)
Exam TipMust remain in original position — disturbed features cannot be dated. Less commonly tested but conceptually interesting for MCQs on "what material"
💡

OSL — Optically Stimulated Luminescence

Absolute
MaterialSediment grains (quartz, feldspar) — dates when sediment was last exposed to sunlight
PrincipleLight resets electron traps in grains. After burial, traps refill from radiation. Expose to light in lab — emitted light = accumulated dose = age since burial
Range100 to ~350,000 years BP
Exam TipUsed to date when sediments were deposited — crucial for sites beyond C-14 range. Used at Jebel Irhoud (Morocco) to date the 315,000 BP H. sapiens remains.

The Ultimate Dating Method Quick Test: When a question gives you a material and asks which method — ask: Is it organic? → C-14. Volcanic rock? → K-Ar. Pottery/fired clay? → TL. Cave formations/coral? → U-series. Wood? → Dendrochronology. Sediment? → OSL. Bone comparing same deposit? → FUN. All of these distinctions appear in UGC NET papers.

Structuralism (Lévi-Strauss) 1950s–1970s Claude Lévi-Strauss; influenced by Saussure, Jakobson (linguistics) Universal binary oppositions underlie all culture (raw/cooked, nature/culture, hot/cold). The savage mind operates by bricolage. Myths and kinship share deep structure. Structural analysis of myths, kinship, totemism — find the underlying logical structure; not empiricist Elementary Structures of Kinship (1949); Mythologiques (4 vols, 1964–71); Savage Mind (1962) Too abstract and untestable; ignores history and social context; critics say binary oppositions are imposed by analyst, not found in data
Symbolic / Interpretive 1960s–1980s Clifford Geertz, Victor Turner, Mary Douglas Culture as a system of symbols and meanings; thick description (Geertz); symbols are multivocal (Turner); culture as text to be interpreted Interpretive approach; ethnography as translation; symbolic analysis of ritual, art, religion Interpretation of Cultures (Geertz 1973); The Ritual Process (Turner 1969); Purity & Danger (Douglas 1966) Relativistic; cannot compare cultures scientifically; "whose interpretation?" problem; too focused on meaning at expense of material conditions
Neo-Evolutionism 1940s–1960s Leslie White, Julian Steward, Marshall Sahlins, Elman Service White: culture evolves via energy capture (E×T→C). Steward: multilinear evolution; cultural ecology. Service: Band→Tribe→Chiefdom→State Cross-cultural comparison; materialist and ecological analysis; energy and technology focus Science of Culture (White 1949); Theory of Culture Change (Steward 1955); Stone Age Economics (Sahlins 1972) White is too deterministic; ignores agency; Steward's cultural ecology sometimes circular; Service's typology oversimplifies political diversity
Marxist Anthropology 1960s–1980s Meillassoux, Terray, Godelier, Eric Wolf, Eleanor Leacock Mode of production shapes social relations; kinship as ideology masking exploitation; domestic mode of production in pre-capitalist societies; class analysis applied cross-culturally Historical materialist analysis; political economy framework; attention to labour, reproduction, inequality Europe & People Without History (Wolf 1982); Maidens Meal & Money (Meillassoux 1975) Economic reductionism; neglects symbolism and meaning; European Marxist categories may not translate cross-culturally
Feminist Anthropology 1970s–present Rosaldo, Ortner, Leacock, Slocum, Eleanor Burke Leacock, Strathern Anthropology has been androcentric; woman–nature::man–culture (Ortner); woman the gatherer hypothesis challenges male-biased narratives; intersectionality Critical review of existing ethnography; feminist fieldwork methodology; focus on women's perspectives; reflexive ethnography Woman Culture & Society (Rosaldo & Lamphere 1974); Making Gender (Ortner 1996) Early feminist anthropology accused of universalising "woman"; later postcolonial feminists (Mohanty) critique Western feminist assumptions about Third World women
Practice Theory / Agency 1970s–present Pierre Bourdieu, Anthony Giddens, Marshall Sahlins, Sherry Ortner Bridge between structure and agency; habitus (Bourdieu); structuration (Giddens); people both constrained by and reproduce social structures through daily practices Ethnographic observation of everyday practice; attention to how structure is reproduced or transformed through action Outline of a Theory of Practice (Bourdieu 1977); Distinction (1984); Making History (Sahlins) Bourdieu's habitus sometimes circular; not fully able to account for radical social change; agency can be romanticised
Postmodern / Reflexive 1980s–2000s James Clifford, George Marcus, Michael Fischer, Paul Rabinow Crisis of representation; ethnographic authority questioned; texts are partial, positioned, and political; writing is a form of power; multi-sited ethnography Reflexive ethnography; dialogue and polyphony in texts; attention to power in fieldwork; experimental writing Writing Culture (Clifford & Marcus 1986); Anthropology as Cultural Critique (Marcus & Fischer) Paralyses fieldwork with doubt; produces impenetrable writing; accused of solipsism; ironically still produced by privileged Western academics
Transactionalism / Processualism 1960s–1980s Fredrik Barth, Nick Bailey, Jeremy Boissevain Focus on individuals as strategists maximising values; ethnic boundaries maintained by interaction, not primordial traits; social organisation as outcome of individual decisions Focus on processes, strategies, networks; transaction as unit of analysis; case studies of decision-making Models of Social Organisation (Barth 1966); Ethnic Groups & Boundaries (Barth 1969) Overly individualistic; rational-actor model ignores structural constraints; neglects meaning and emotion; difficult to scale up to macro analysis

Sentinelese★★ALWAYS IN EXAM
North Sentinel Island (A&N)
Most isolated humans on Earth; reject all outside contact; Indian Navy maintains exclusion zone (3 nautical miles); 2018 — John Allen Chau missionary killed. Population unknown (~50–500). Protected by Indian govt under Andaman & Nicobar Protection of Aboriginal Tribes Act.

Great Andamanese★
Strait Island (A&N Islands)
Population: ~60 (near extinction)
Originally 10 tribes; decimated by British colonisation and disease after 1858; from ~5,000 to ~60 today. Now settled on Strait Island. Classic example of contact-induced depopulation.
Shompen★
Great Nicobar Island
Population: ~200–300
Interior forest dwellers of Great Nicobar; semi-nomadic; avoid outsiders; threatened by Great Nicobar development project (2022) — major conservation controversy. Hunter-gatherers + forest food.
